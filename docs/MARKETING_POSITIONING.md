# Minty Marketing Positioning

Source inspiration: Edward Sturm’s Steve Jobs / Got Milk marketing lesson. Great marketing sells the emotionally obvious moment, not the feature category.

## Marketing principle

Do not lead with “AI relationship memory,” “private network search,” or “CRM for people.” Those are product shapes. Lead with the moment that hurts:

> You know the person you need is already somewhere in your network. You just cannot find the path in time.

Minty should sell recall, leverage, and hidden access, not database hygiene.

## Core story

People do not fail at networking because they lack contacts. They fail because their network is fragmented across Telegram, WhatsApp, Slack, LinkedIn, email, notes, and memory.

Minty turns scattered relationship evidence into useful answers:

1. Who do I know around this problem?
2. Why are they relevant?
3. How warm is the relationship?
4. What is the safest next step?

## Plain-language hooks

- “Your network already knows the answer. Minty helps you find the path.”
- “Stop asking ‘who do I know?’ from memory.”
- “The person you need is probably two searches away, if your network memory works.”
- “Private relationship memory for moments when timing matters.”

## Buyer/user pain to dramatize

The painful moment:

> You are about to ask for an intro, sell a customer, raise money, hire someone, or research a market, and you vaguely remember that somebody knows somebody, but the trail is buried across years of messages.

Marketing should make this feel specific: a founder trying to find a warm path before sending a cold email.

## What to avoid

- Do not make Minty sound like another CRM.
- Do not lead with “AI agent” or “LLM memory.” That is implementation, not value.
- Do not imply contact details are exposed by default. Privacy and redaction are part of the trust story.
- Do not sell broad “knowledge management.” Sell high-stakes relationship recall.

## Good landing page structure

1. Headline: Find the warm path already hidden in your network.
2. Subhead: Minty gives you private, source-backed relationship memory across your messages and contacts, with safe suggested next steps.
3. Pain section: forgotten intros, fragmented inboxes, stale CRMs, missed timing.
4. Product proof: ask a goal, get relevant people, evidence, warmth, suggested action.
5. CTA: “Search your network memory.”

---

This note is intentionally positioning-first: lead with the buyer's painful moment, identity, and relief before naming product features.
